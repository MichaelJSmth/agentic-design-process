§ 00   Introduction

By Michael JaQuan Smith · 2026

AI changes how design moves, not what design is.

Today, 91% of designers use AI in their work each week. The average uses seven tools to do so (AI in Design 2026).

The brief still needs framing. The work still needs taste. The ship decision still belongs to a person.

What changes is the loop. Generation, critique, and exploration that used to take days now take minutes. Used well, this compresses the path between problem and product. Used poorly, it builds the wrong thing faster than anyone else can.

Use this process when the problem is ambiguous, the behavior is the design, and the risk of being wrong is real. AI features, agents, automation, complex flows, search, recommendations, and decision support.

Skip it for visual polish, component cleanup, copy changes, or work where the answer is already known.

Operating note

Use the smallest version that improves the decision. Skip any step that doesn't change the work.

AI generates, drafts, critiques, and stress-tests. People frame, clarify, decide, craft, accept risk, and ship.

§ 01   The Loop

Eight moves that move backward when evidence demands.

01   Chapter One

Direction

Before you make anything, get the decision right. Four moves that determine whether the work has any chance of being useful.

1.1Frame

Every project should be anchored to the decision it needs to move. Find it before you open the model.

A brief is not a decision. It is an invitation to confuse the solution with the request. Name the user, the problem, the evidence, the unknowns, the constraints, and what success looks like. Write it in one sentence.

The model will solve anything you give it. Give it the wrong problem and it will solve that beautifully.

TestIf you can describe the problem without naming the solution, the frame is good.

1.2Clarify

Don't let the agent design from ambiguity silently.

Make it expose what it doesn't know before it touches the design. A good agent identifies the decisions that would change the design, asks the smallest useful set of questions, and states assumptions when answers are missing.

Clarification is a filter, not a meeting. Ask only what changes direction, behavior, risk, fidelity, or implementation. If a question doesn't affect the work, skip it.

1.3Ground

AI is a stranger to your product. It reasons about a generic version of your problem unless you stop it.

Give it your research, your screenshots, your analytics, your support signals, your design system rules, your content voice, your technical constraints, your reference library, and examples of what to follow and what to avoid. Build this context stack once and reuse it across the project.

Strong inputs beat long prompts. A model with no context produces generic best practices. A model with your context produces work that could only belong to your product.

1.4Explore

Before designing UI, generate three to five directions that differ in strategy, not layout.

Explore broadly before drafting deeply. Don't let code or a polished first draft make the earliest idea feel more certain than it is.

The temptation with AI is to ship the first plausible idea. Plausibility is not quality. Compare directions by user value, business value, interaction model, risk, and the fastest way to kill or confirm each.

WarningIf the options only differ in arrangement, you haven't explored. You've decorated.

02   Chapter Two

Building

How work actually moves from concept to shipped. Behavior, drafts, prototypes, craft, critique, validation, and what to leave behind.

2.1Model behavior

For AI features, behavior is the design. Screens are the interface to the behavior.

Specify what the system infers, what it asks, what it explains, what it refuses, what it escalates, and what the user can undo. Cover normal use, failure, and recovery. Write it before you open Figma.

A beautiful interface to a confused system still feels confused.

2.2Draft first

After a direction is chosen and the agent has clarified what matters, ask it to create a rough first draft.

The draft should make the idea discussable, not prematurely final. Treat it as a provocation the designer can judge, redirect, and improve.

The goal isn't the answer. It's something specific enough to react to: a rough flow, wireframe, screen structure, component hierarchy, coded sketch, or state map.

A good first draft makes the work discussable. A bad first draft still reveals assumptions. The designer's job is to judge, edit, redirect, and raise the quality bar.

2.3Prototype

Match fidelity to the question. Agentic doesn't mean code-first.

Use the tool that answers the question. Text answers framing. Figma answers direction and structure. Code answers behavior. A playground answers believability. Production answers feasibility.

QuestionBest surface
Is the idea clear?Text
Does the direction feel right?Figma
Can the user complete the flow?Clickable prototype
Does the behavior feel believable?Code prototype or playground
Can engineering build it?Branch, spike, or production path

Tool choice determines what kind of answer you can trust. It's not a workflow preference.

The wrong fidelity wastes time twice: once making it, once explaining why it doesn't answer the question. A prototype that needs narration isn't done.

2.4Craft

Craft is the part of the work AI compresses least.

Use the model to generate options, expose gaps, and push coverage. Then manually tune the final ten percent: interaction details, layout rhythm, motion, CSS, responsive behavior, accessibility, and the parts of the product that need taste.

Agents can move the work forward. They still miss the details that make a product feel intentional.

Quality thresholdWould this still feel good if it had taken a week to make?

2.5Critique

Critique before stakeholder review. AI runs the first pass.

Have it act as a senior designer, a researcher, an accessibility lead, a design systems owner, a staff engineer, a skeptical PM, or a first-time user. One persona at a time. Specific failures, severity, smallest useful fix.

"Make it cleaner" isn't feedback. "The empty state has no path forward" is.

2.6Validate

"This feels good" is not validation. It is a feeling.

Test for usability, accessibility, state coverage, feasibility, risk, and measurement. The goal isn't proving the work is perfect. It's knowing what risk you're accepting.

Every blocker has an owner. Every accepted risk is written down. Without both, validation is theater.

2.7Multiplayer checkpoint

AI makes it easier for everyone to generate work in isolation. Before the work moves forward, make sure the team knows the source of truth, who owns the decision, what changed since the last review, what's ready for critique, what isn't, and where PM, engineering, research, and design need to weigh in.

2.8Ship

Ship a package, not a mockup.

Flows. States. Components. AI behavior rules. Analytics events. Risks. Open questions. Clear about what can flex and what must not change.

If engineering has to reverse-engineer the intent from the pixels, the handoff failed.

2.9Learn

Every project should leave better context behind it than it found.

When you can, use the thing you're designing. Dogfooding exposes gaps that reviews and mocks often miss. After launch, update the grounding stack. Save what worked. Save what failed. Save the prompts, the design rules, the edge cases, and the lessons.

If a workflow repeats, turn it into a reusable tool: a prompt, skill file, script, checklist, reference library, prototype component, or microtool.

The next project should start further along than this one did.

03   Chapter Three

Practice

Operating principles that keep the process from becoming faster theater, and the short list of things designers don't get to delegate.

3.1   Operating Principles

These rules keep agentic work from collapsing into the first plausible output.

  1. 01

    AI is not a step

    Use it across the loop: framing, clarifying, drafting, critique, QA, documentation, and handoff.

  2. 02

    Breadth before depth

    Do not let code, polish, or a convincing first draft commit you to the first plausible idea.

  3. 03

    Drafts are provocations

    A draft makes an idea discussable. Treat it that way. Not as the answer.

  4. 04

    Tool choice is truth

    Text, Figma, code, playgrounds, and production each answer different questions.

  5. 05

    Quality is human-owned

    Never accept AI output because it's plausible. Accept it only when it clears the product's quality bar.

  6. 06

    Save the system

    Save prompts, context, references, critique rubrics, microtools, and rules. Not just the final artifact.

3.2   What Never Gets Delegated

  • 01Final judgment
  • 02Taste
  • 03Ethics
  • 04Stakeholder alignment
  • 05Live research decisions
  • 06Product strategy
  • 07The decision of what risk to accept

Designers don't only produce screens. They shape the system that produces screens.

The designer owns the quality bar, the context stack, the critique loop, and the rules that keep AI-generated work aligned with the product.

These are the parts of design that don't compress.
