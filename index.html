How to design with AI, not around it.

A working process for ambiguous problems, real behavior, and the moments that decide whether the product is good.

Michael JaQuan Smith · Senior Product Designer
Begin reading →

§ 00   Introduction

By Michael JaQuan Smith · 2026

AI changes how design moves, not what design is.

In a year, weekly AI use among designers went from 54% to 91%, and the average designer's toolkit grew from three tools to seven. Independent data agrees: the most-used design tool after Figma is now an AI. Production is no longer the bottleneck.

That's the whole point, and the whole danger. When anyone can generate a plausible screen in a minute, the scarce thing is no longer output. It's judgment. A model will hand you something that looks finished long before the problem is understood.

So the brief still needs framing. The work still needs taste. The ship decision still belongs to a person. What changes is the loop: generation, critique, and exploration that used to take days now take minutes.

This is a process for that loop. If you know the double diamond, you already know its shape: diverge then converge, twice. Once on the problem, once on the solution.

Use it when the problem is ambiguous, the behavior is the design, and the cost of being wrong is real: AI features, agents, automation, complex flows, search, recommendations, and decision support.

Skip it for visual polish, component cleanup, copy tweaks, or work where the answer is already known. Not every task needs a process.

Operating note

Use the smallest version that improves the decision. Skip any move that wouldn't change the work.

§ 01   The Loop

Nine moves, two diamonds.

Move only as far as the decision requires, and move backward the moment evidence changes it.

  1. 01 · Direction

    Frame

    Name the decision the work has to move. Make the agent expose what it doesn't know.

  2. 02 · Direction

    Ground

    Give the model your context, not generic best practice.

  3. 03 · Direction

    Explore

    Diverge into three to five strategic directions. Then converge.

  4. 04 · Building

    Model behavior

    For AI features, design the behavior before the screens.

  5. 05 · Building

    Draft

    A rough first cut, specific enough to react to.

  6. 06 · Building

    Prototype

    Match the fidelity to the question you're asking.

  7. 07 · Building

    Craft

    The last increment AI compresses least.

  8. 08 · Building

    Critique & validate

    AI runs the first critique. Then test coverage, access, feasibility, risk, and evals.

  9. 09 · Building

    Ship & learn

    Hand off a package, not a mockup. Leave better context behind than you found.

The loop isn't linear. The two diamonds are ordered, but inside them you move backward whenever new evidence demands it. A failing critique sends you back to draft. A direction that won't validate sends you back to explore. New evidence should change direction, not decorate it.

Worked example

AI-drafted replies in a customer-support inbox: the agent reads a ticket and proposes a reply the human agent can edit or send. It is a useful test case because the behavior, not the screen, is the design.

§ 02   The Process

Diverge and converge twice.

Two phases, then a practice layer that holds across every move.

Phase One   Direction

Before you make anything, get the decision right. Three moves decide whether the work has any chance of being useful.

01Frame

Anchor the work to the decision it has to move before you open the model.

A brief is not a decision. It's an invitation to confuse the solution with the request. Name the user, the problem, the evidence, the unknowns, the constraints, and what success looks like. Then write the problem in one sentence.

Then make the agent clarify before it designs. A good agent names the decisions that would change the design, asks the smallest useful set of questions, and states its assumptions where answers are missing.

Test: if you can describe the problem without naming the solution, the frame is good.

Done when: you have a one-sentence problem statement with no solution in it, and the agent's hidden assumptions are on the table.

Template: 01 Project snapshot · 07 Frame the problem · 08 Clarify before designing

In practice

Brief: "Add AI-suggested replies to the support inbox." That names a solution, not a problem. Reframed: "Support agents spend the first 40 seconds of every ticket re-reading context and typing the same openings; we want to cut time-to-first-reply without lowering answer quality." The agent asks two questions that matter: can a suggestion be sent without edits, and what happens when the model is unsure?

02Ground

AI is a stranger to your product. It reasons about a generic version of your problem unless you stop it.

Give it your research, screenshots, analytics, support signals, design-system rules, content voice, technical constraints, reference library, and clear examples of what to follow and avoid. This is also where the non-negotiables live: privacy, consent, data provenance, and what the system is and isn't allowed to do on a user's behalf.

Principle: strong inputs beat long prompts.

Done when: you could hand the grounding stack to a new teammate and they'd produce on-brand, on-constraint work without asking you anything.

Template: 02 Grounding stack

In practice

Into the stack: real anonymized tickets, the support voice guide, the rule that AI may draft but a refund over $50 needs human approval, and the constraint that ticket content can't leave the EU region. Generic best practice would have designed one-click auto-send. The grounding rules it out.

03Explore

Diverge before you converge. Generate three to five directions that differ in strategy, not layout.

The temptation with AI is to ship the first plausible idea. Plausibility is not quality, and a polished first draft makes the earliest idea feel more certain than it is. Force genuine difference, then compare directions by user value, business value, interaction model, risk, and the fastest way to kill or confirm each.

Warning: if the options only differ in arrangement, you haven't explored. You've decorated.

Done when: you have three to five strategically distinct directions, a chosen one, and a one-line reason each loser lost.

Template: 09 Generate directions

In practice

Three real directions, not three layouts: inline ghost-text the agent accepts with Tab, a drafted reply in a side panel the agent edits before sending, or a triage layer that suggests reply, escalate, or close before drafting. We pick the side panel: highest control, lowest blast radius if the model is wrong.

Phase Two   Building

How the work moves from a chosen direction to something shipped. Behavior, drafts, prototypes, craft, critique, validation, and what you leave behind.

04Model behavior

For AI features, behavior is the design. Screens are just the interface to the behavior.

Specify what the system infers, what it asks, what it explains, what it refuses, what it escalates, and what the user can undo. Cover the normal path, the failure path, and the recovery path. Design for variance: the same input won't always produce the same output, and behavior drifts when the model is updated.

Principle: a beautiful interface to a confused system still feels confused.

Done when: normal, failure, recovery, and low-confidence behavior are all specified, including what the AI must not do, and you've written what "a good output" means concretely enough to test.

Template: 03 Behavior model · 11 Model behavior

In practice

The drafted-reply agent infers intent from the ticket and account history. It flags "low confidence: review carefully" below a threshold, refuses to promise refunds over $50, explains its draft with "based on: order number, refund policy," and drafts nothing when no relevant context is available.

05Draft

Ask for a rough first cut. Its job is to be discussable, not final.

Once the direction is chosen and the agent has clarified what matters, get something specific on the table fast: a rough flow, wireframe, screen structure, coded sketch, or state map. Treat it as a provocation, not the answer.

Principle: keep it rough. Optimize for clarity, not polish.

Done when: the team can react to something specific. Not when it looks done.

Template: 10 Create a rough first draft

In practice

The first draft is a flow and five labeled states: side panel with a draft, edited state, low-confidence state, escalation state, and empty state. It's ugly. It immediately surfaces the real question: what does the panel show before the agent has read the ticket?

06Prototype

Match the fidelity to the question. Agentic doesn't mean code-first.

Use the surface that answers the question you actually have. The wrong fidelity wastes time twice: once making it, once explaining why it doesn't answer the question.

QuestionBest surface
Is the idea clear?Text
Does the direction feel right?Figma
Can the user complete the flow?Clickable prototype
Does the behavior feel believable?Code prototype or playground
Can engineering build it?Branch, spike, or production path

Test: a prototype that needs narration isn't done.

Done when: the prototype answers its question on its own.

In practice

"Does an AI-drafted reply feel trustworthy enough to send?" is a believability question, so Figma can't answer it. We build a code prototype wired to the real model on sample tickets to feel the latency, tone, and trust moment.

07Craft

Craft is the part of the work AI compresses least.

Use the model to generate options, expose gaps, and push coverage. Then tune what it can't: interaction detail, layout rhythm, motion, responsive behavior, accessibility, the copy that carries trust, and the microcopy on the low-confidence state.

Quality threshold: would this still feel good if it had taken a week to make?

Done when: it clears your product's bar, not the model's "looks plausible" bar.

In practice

The model's draft-reply UI is fine. The craft is in the exact wording of "Review carefully: I'm not sure about this one," the 150ms the draft takes to appear, and the focus order so a keyboard user lands on Edit, not Send.

08Critique & validate

AI runs the first critique. Then you validate against reality.

Have the model act as a senior designer, researcher, accessibility lead, design-systems owner, staff engineer, skeptical PM, or first-time user. One persona at a time, each naming specific failures, severity, and the smallest useful fix.

Then validate usability, accessibility, state coverage, feasibility, cost, latency, and risk. For AI behavior, that means evals: run real and adversarial inputs, score outputs against the definition of good, and keep that set so you can catch regressions when the model changes.

Principle: every blocker has an owner. Every accepted risk is written down.

Done when: critique personas have run, the eval set passes at an agreed bar, and every remaining risk has a name next to it.

Template: 04 Validation checklist · 12 Critique the work

In practice

The skeptical PM asks what happens at 10x ticket volume on Monday, surfacing a latency cost no one had priced. The eval set is 50 tickets, including difficult cases. The draft is acceptable on 44. We ship only because a human edits every reply, and we write that accepted risk down.

09Ship & learn

Hand off a package, not a mockup, then leave the project better-documented than you found it.

Before the work moves, run the team checkpoint. Agree on the source of truth, who owns the decision, what changed since the last review, what's ready for critique, and where PM, engineering, and research need to weigh in.

Then ship the package: flows, states, components, AI behavior rules, the eval set, analytics events, known risks, and open questions. After launch, use the thing you designed, then feed the grounding stack with what worked, what failed, the prompts, rules, edge cases, and evals.

Principle: the next project should start further along than this one did.

Done when: engineering can build it without asking what you meant, and the grounding stack is richer than when you started.

Template: 05 Handoff note · 06 Reusable assets

In practice

The handoff includes the behavior model, the 50-ticket eval set, the 44/50 bar, the named risk, analytics events, and one line in bold: do not enable one-click send without re-running the eval set. Two weeks later, discarded rate shows the low-confidence flag is firing too often, and that finding goes back into the stack.

Practice   Layer

Rules across every move

  1. 01

    AI is not a step

    Use it across the whole loop: framing, clarifying, drafting, critique, QA, documentation, and handoff.

  2. 02

    Breadth before depth

    Don't let code, polish, or a convincing first draft commit you to the first plausible idea.

  3. 03

    Drafts are provocations

    A draft makes an idea discussable. Treat it that way, not as the answer.

  4. 04

    Tool choice is truth

    Text, Figma, code, playgrounds, and production each answer different questions. Choosing the surface is choosing what kind of answer you can trust.

  5. 05

    Quality is human-owned

    Never accept output because it's plausible. Accept it only when it clears the product's bar.

  6. 06

    Design for variance

    The model is non-deterministic. Judge the distribution of outputs, not one lucky run, and re-check when the model changes.

  7. 07

    Design is still multiplayer

    AI makes solo generation easy and silent. Default to a shared source of truth and a visible decision owner.

  8. 08

    Save the system

    Keep prompts, context, references, critique rubrics, eval sets, and rules. Not just the final artifact.

Never   Delegated

  • 01Final judgment
  • 02Taste
  • 03Ethics
  • 04Stakeholder alignment
  • 05Live research decisions
  • 06Product strategy
  • 07The decision of what risk to accept

Designers don't only produce screens. They shape the system that produces screens: the quality bar, the context stack, the critique loop, the eval set, and the rules that keep AI-generated work aligned with the product.

These are the parts of design that don't compress.

Role   Shift

The designer's changing role

The job is shifting from making the artifact to directing the system that makes it. Three capabilities are becoming the differentiators: context engineering, specifying and evaluating behavior, and taste under abundance.

None of these is new to design. AI just moved them from the edges of the job to the center of it.

04 Validation checklist +

Run before shipping. Anything unchecked is either a known risk or an unfinished section.

  • The screen reads in five seconds.
  • The flow completes without explanation.
  • Default, loading, empty, error, and success states are designed.
  • The user knows what AI is doing.
  • The user can undo, edit, or override.
  • AI failure modes are handled.
  • The AI behavior passes the eval set at the agreed bar.
  • The eval set includes adversarial / edge-case inputs.
  • Output is acceptable across repeated runs, not just once.
  • Cost and latency are acceptable at expected volume.
  • There is a plan to re-validate when the model changes.
  • The design reuses the system.
  • It works on mobile.
  • It meets the accessibility floor.
  • Engineering can build it reasonably.
  • The work can be measured.
  • The accepted risk is named.
  • The source of truth is clear.
  • The team knows what changed and what needs review.
